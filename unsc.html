TOPIC - Addressing the situation in Somaliland

After the independence from the British and subsequent union with Somalia in 1960, Somaliland was a prospering region.

However, the South of Somalia quickly dominated the North, leading to the marginalisation of Somaliland and the subsequent anti-union movement. Several revolutions were attempted, although most failed due to the small number of soldiers and the lack of military preparation.

Nonetheless, in 1991 the Somali National Movement, led by Abdirahman Ahmed Ali Tuur, declared independence for all territories of Somaliland.

In 2001, the constitution of Somaliland was also voted upon, passing with an overwhelming majority.

While all of this happened, the main problem for Somaliland remains international recognition. Currently no country recognises Somaliland as an independent nation. This comes during a time when the territory has its own constitution, president and other factors that would make any state in their situation a de facto state.

About the Committee

The Security Council has primary responsibility for the maintenance of international peace and security. It has 15 Members, and each Member has one vote. Under the Charter of the United Nations, all Member States are obligated to comply with Council decisions. The Security Council takes the lead in determining the existence of a threat to the peace or act of aggression.
